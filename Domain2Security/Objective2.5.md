# 2.5 Social Engineering Attacks, Threats, and Vulnerabilities

# Social Engineering

## Phishing
Fraudulent attempt to steal information through deceptive emails or messages.  
Example: Fake bank email requesting password reset.

### Vishing
Voice-based phishing attack conducted over phone calls.  
Example: Fake IT support calling for login credentials.

### Smishing
Phishing attack conducted through SMS/text messages.  
Example: Fake delivery text containing malicious link.

### QR Code Phishing
Attack using malicious QR codes directing victims to fake websites.  
Example: Scanning fake parking-payment QR code.

### Spear Phishing
Targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization.  
Example: Fake email sent specifically to company accountant.

### Whaling
Spear phishing attack targeting high-level executives or VIPs.  
Example: CEO receiving fake legal subpoena email.

---

## Shoulder Surfing
Stealing information by watching someone enter sensitive data.  
Example: Watching user type ATM PIN.

## Tailgating
Unauthorized person physically following authorized user into secure area.  
Example: Attacker entering office behind employee without badge.

## Impersonation
Pretending to be someone trusted to gain access or information.  
Example: Attacker posing as help desk technician.

## Dumpster Diving
Searching discarded materials for sensitive information.  
Example: Finding passwords in thrown-away documents.

---

# Threats

## Denial of Service (DoS)
Attack attempting to make systems or services unavailable to users.  
Example: Flooding server with excessive requests.

## Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)
DoS attack launched simultaneously from many compromised devices.  
Example: Botnet attacking company website.

## Evil Twin
Fake wireless access point imitating legitimate Wi-Fi network.  
Example: Fake coffee shop Wi-Fi stealing credentials.

## Zero-day Attack
Attack exploiting unknown vulnerability before vendor releases patch.  
Example: Malware exploiting newly discovered Windows flaw.

## Spoofing
Falsifying identity information to appear as trusted source.  
Example: Fake sender email address appearing legitimate.

## On-path Attack
Attacker secretly intercepts communication between two parties.  
Example: Attacker capturing traffic on public Wi-Fi.

## Brute-force Attack
Attack attempting every possible password combination.  
Example: Automated password guessing attempts.

## Dictionary Attack
Attack using common words and passwords from predefined lists.  
Example: Trying passwords like “password123” and “welcome”.

## Insider Threat
Security risk originating from authorized internal users.  
Example: Employee stealing confidential company files.

## Structured Query Language (SQL) Injection
Attack inserting malicious SQL commands into application input fields.  
Example: Bypassing website login using malicious SQL query.

## Cross-site Scripting (XSS)
Attack injecting malicious scripts into trusted webpages.  
Example: Malicious JavaScript executing in users’ browsers.

## Business Email Compromise (BEC)
Attack impersonating business executives to trick employees into sending money or data.  
Example: Fake CEO email requesting urgent wire transfer.

## Supply Chain/Pipeline Attack
Attack targeting trusted suppliers or software providers to compromise victims indirectly.  
Example: Malware distributed through compromised software update.

# Vulnerabilities

## Non-compliant Systems
Systems failing to meet required security policies or standards.  
Example: Workstation violating company password policy.

## Unpatched Systems
Systems missing important security updates or patches.  
Example: Server vulnerable due to outdated operating system.

## Unprotected Systems (Missing Antivirus/Missing Firewall)
Systems lacking basic security protections against threats.  
Example: PC running without antivirus software enabled.

## EOL (End of Life)
Hardware or software no longer receiving vendor support or updates.  
Example: Windows 7 system after support ended.

## Bring Your Own Device (BYOD)
Policy allowing personal devices to access company resources.  
Example: Employee using personal smartphone for work email.
